Introduction

E-learning enables flexibility in learning, which encourages the highest quality professional training. All learners can access the same curriculum, learning resources, and training through e-learning. By utilising e-learning, you may save time and money and reduce commuting expenditures. As a result, e-learning is more affordable than traditional education. A successful e-learning course should consist of high-quality content, a professional design, and intuitive navigation. However, one of the most critical parts of designing and implementing eLearning programmes is being overlooked, and that is interaction. Through audiovisual, film, dialogue, and engagement, web-based learning enables educators to dig into this world of continuous learning. As a result, educators can successfully integrate theoretical topics into the classroom through everyday practices. It is vital for institutes of higher learning to utilise contemporary internet technology in the classroom. It illustrates some of the global perspectives expressed by individuals and organisations through interviews and other research. It examines the numerous interpretations of e-learning offered by academics, the role of e-learning in universities and colleges’ teaching and learning activities, and the benefits and risks associated with its adoption and implementation. This review will undertake a stand-alone literature analysis and provide a scientific foundation by examining a few of the contributions made by several researchers and learning institutions to the notion of e-learning, particularly its usage in higher educational institutions.

Use of Technology

The e-learning programme has a competitive advantage over the traditional method. E-learning is rapidly gaining ground in the sphere of education. As a result, eLearning is crucial in educational institutions because it provides greater autonomy and versatility in time, location, physical engagement, and challenging and busy teaching-learning. Nonetheless, several difficulties must be addressed before E-Learning may be used effectively as a teaching-learning tool (Quadri et al., 2017).

Before implementing e-learning, it is necessary to design and maintain an operational data innovation plan. Numerous vital success elements for e-learning may assist schools and professors in utilising e-learning technology more safely and effectively (Tawafak et al., 2019). Learners’ perceptions of the qualities of pedagogical distribution media and their capacity to study them are significant predictors of their aspirations and performance in a traditional classroom setting (Coggins, 2018; Gee, 2020). These impressions may be valid when innovative techniques serve as the primary data flow source to learners in computer-mediated educational settings. Only a handful of research studies have discovered a correlation between studying personality and style and technological advancements.

Abuhassna & Yahaya (2018) discovered that respondents to their alternative evaluations had a negative attitude toward electronics. They argued that while computer-assisted training may be an excellent alternative for learners whose study habits are practical and testing and evaluation-oriented, introspective students may feel awkward and disappointed when exposed to this teaching style. On the other hand, Abuhassna et al. (2020) discovered that “pupils with a greater interest in objects or things (concrete experience) and a decreased interest in dealing with people” learned more effectively when using computer-assisted education.

Alalwan et al. (2019)’s hypothesis proposed that students use a “bag of tricks” to gain an understanding of unfamiliar surroundings. This “bag of tricks” includes previous training methods, techniques, and elements that worked in other settings. According to Allen et al. (2016), the past teachable moment is critical for learners to adjust to digital training, among other things.

According to Al-Rahmi et al. (2018)’s research project on using “Asynchronous Learning Networks” to educate learners at two different universities, the information communication technologies included in the programme posed barriers to education for those without prior knowledge of the technical characteristics.

Perhaps one of their recommendations for online education is to avoid simultaneously educating the technique and the programme but rather impose requirements on the techniques used throughout the programme or offer a mini-course on the innovations integrated into the curriculum. Additionally, scientists assert that the effective deployment of any technological advancement is contingent upon user beliefs and perceptions (Arkorful & Abaidoo, 2022). For instance, Azhari & Ming (2015) examined the efficacy of instruction in technology-mediated online classes and discovered a positive correlation between learners’ perceptions of technology and learning outcomes. Thus, understanding technology and how to use it are critical outcomes of web-based learning.

Security Consideration

Because more people are taking online classes, industries must be aware of the security risks associated with e-learning. Corporations that educate their employees via e-learning systems should pay close attention to safety concerns (Barkand, 2017).

As online education has grown in popularity and the Internet has developed into a new venue for various nefarious practices, e-learning environments are vulnerable to these threats. This study will examine several of the most common risks associated with online education and potential solutions (Choy & Quek, 2016).

Essential security requirements for e-learning systems include legitimacy, network management, secrecy, consistency, accessibility, and non-repudiation. Security validation is critical for identifying and validating the subscriber’s access permissions to the web-based application. This method prevents attackers from gaining access to another customer’s profile, obtaining confidential information, or committing illegal acts (Falloon, 2016).

Furthermore, once verified, the user will be able to reset his password. Some excellent verification procedures are given below:

• Demanding re-authentication at multiple time points; 

• Internet users should ensure they use secure passwords (containing some upper-case letters, at minimum one-digit, certain special characters, and so on); and 

• Establishing role-based network access.

The accessibility model is done during the verification phase when the learner is given all essential privileges. As a result, the learner will only be able to do his authorised actions in the network (Gouws et al., 2013). 

A role-based authentication paradigm prohibits illegitimate users’ access privileges; it enables the generation of units, the participation of individuals in groups, and the incorporation of factions inside teams. This architecture enables dynamic and detailed governance over each user’s permissions. Permits to conduct specified activities are delegated to particular positions. Staff employees (or other network administrators) are allocated specific responsibilities, and as a result, they have access to specific functional units. Users do not have privileges allocated to them explicitly but instead get them via their role (s), so managing individual user privileges becomes a simple issue of giving learners suitable functions. 

A general pattern’s secrecy should be protected via secure data transfer and application. The authenticity of files and documents is an important issue often overlooked. Only approved entities (people or automated systems) can alter data (or executable systems). Data privacy is intertwined with algorithmic and platform stability. The benchmark panel may stop working if the underlying program’s integrity is compromised. The benchmark screen ensures that only authenticated users can access information and perform actions. Data security is compromised without this method of checking and limiting internet connectivity. As a result, maintaining operational system stability is critical for data security (Edgar, 2005).

Backup and recovery across multiple sites is another intelligent security strategy. The duplication procedure improves programme security while increasing data processing efficiency (Iacob, 2010).

Non-repudiation indicates that users cannot credibly refute performing activities. Suppose an instructor erases his or her learners’ test scores. It should be easy to utilise “log files” and determine who removed them in this situation. Furthermore, these “log files” should be dependable and tamper-proof. The technique utilised to meet these criteria is monitoring. A biometric system is another non-repudiation contingency plan.      

Privacy & legislation in E-Learning

Academic institutions and other businesses are shifting to e-learning to deliver education via the Internet. Nonetheless, the requirement for “privacy and security” has historically been largely overlooked. The “Privacy Principles” are used to evaluate the privacy and security requirements of e-learning systems. The prospects for several alternative privacy-enhancing strategies are studied and analysed, including techniques for internet confidentiality, policy-based cybersecurity governance, and trustworthy networks (May et al., 2012).

Security breach incidents have prompted governments worldwide to increase public awareness of confidentiality and enact privacy laws and regulations to prevent the commercialisation of private information.

Personal balance is required to use private details in countries with privacy laws, such as the acquisition, use, dissemination, maintenance, and deletion of private data by entities that may manage such data. The term “privacy principles” was coined to emphasise the ramifications of privacy legislation or privacy policies adopted by online firms. One way to determine if software complies with privacy rules effectively is to analyse it in light of “the Privacy Principles” (Kauffman, 2015). Researchers may employ any or all of these “Privacy Principles” in assessing the utility of proposed “Privacy Enhancing Technologies (PET) for e-learning objectives.” While these principles are challenging to execute in any industry, they give a framework for determining the application of technology advances (Privacy Technology Review, 2001).

Remote training and educational levels development will significantly impact the performance of online learning programmes. Quality control and interoperability are crucial for e-learning manufacturers and end-users who wish to sell or purchase handheld output and indistinguishable elements (Kassandrinou et al., 2018).

Additionally, those who are crucial in scenarios involving several e-learning frameworks should communicate with one another. Numerous professional organisations, like “the IEEE Learning Technology Standards Committee (IEEE LTSC),” are now seeking to develop industry-wide specifications (Jacobs et al., 2016).

Although most proposed recommendations concern widely accessible components and educational resources, only a few frameworks and concepts address “privacy and security” concerns in e-learning networks (McAuley et al., 2010).

Advantages of e-learning

Implementing e-learning in schools, particularly on campuses and universities, has several advantages, and as a result of these advantages, e-learning is widely regarded as one of the best modes of education. Numerous academics have argued the advantages and disadvantages of integrating e-learning technologies into colleges (Arkorful & Abaidoo, 2022).

Among the most apparent benefits of online education is the possibility to save “time and money.” Learners have complete control over their work schedule and can take online classes whenever convenient. They are not required to spend money on travel or other incidental charges (May et al., 2012).

E-learning uses a variety of networks, including the dynamic “Pedagogue.” Additionally, students may share their thoughts and comments with others. The more engaging the sessions, the more effectively the kids retain knowledge (Panyajamorn et al., 2018).

Learners have the freedom to learn at their own pace and choose their training schedule. Students will get significantly more involved and motivated by the programme (Lau & Shaikh, 2012).

Numerous academic institutions save money by utilising the eLearning format because it eliminates the requirement for a physical auditorium, resulting in a lower economic cost. (Mathieson, 2012)

E-learning is also less destructive to the environment than paper-based education since it does not generate the toxins associated with paper manufacturing (Pollifroni, 2014).

Disadvantages of e-learning

Finally, we discussed the advantages of online learning. On the Internet, e-learning has numerous significant drawbacks that are often overlooked. Over the years, many researchers have shown the drawbacks of online (Collins et al., 2002). Even though businesses’ projections for 2020 expect significant growth in the E-Learning market. Some systemic difficulties remain (O’Donnell et al., 2013).

E-learning may increase website traffic or usage, causing time and financial waste (Arkorful & Abaidoo, 2022).

Because students no longer engage with their lecturers and peers, e-learning contributes to social alienation E-learning causes social alienation. Interactions are rare or non-existent.

Unfortunately, some of the residents have a limited Internet connection, and they must use public Wi-Fi or internet cafés, which is inconvenient (Salmon, 2014).

Compared to traditional classroom training, e-learning involves evaluation. However, no instructors or scrutineers there to watch students throughout testing. It is easy for internet users to exchange ideas because no one is watching (Stroet et al., 2013).

With e-learning, students are primarily alone. Learners must also learn good time management to balance academic work with other duties such as family chores or part-time work.

Mostly webinars, movies, and slide displays for the learners. The only way to obtain practical experience is to administer exams. Most advanced training approach only need “device and internet connectivity” (Venkatesh et al., 2012). It is, however, not suitable for everyone. Knowing the advantages and disadvantages of e-learning may help one decide if it is good for them.

Conclusion

E-learning is a term that refers to the process of classroom instruction via electronic media. It uses cutting-edge technologies to enable scholars to study at their convenience and from any location. However, technical issues such as “security and privacy” must be solved. Educational standards can be increased by cultivating an environment conducive to student participation (Whitmer, 2013).

The body of research on the benefits and drawbacks of e-learning indicates that its use in postsecondary education is crucial for instructors, executives, and learners to attain the full potential of its development and adoption. E-learning is gaining popularity at the postsecondary level. All available data indicates that recruitment and availability are increasing, albeit from a low base.

Nonetheless, after modern industry’s exuberance, a growing disillusionment with e-learning has substituted over-enthusiasm. At least temporarily, the failures of e-learning activities have obscured the prospects of expanded and more personalised postsecondary education, pedagogical inventiveness, and lower prices that e-learning formerly symbolised (Yu, 2015).

So, where do we stand now that the new economy euphoria has died down? What should more efforts be made to advance e-learning at the postsecondary level and capitalise on its potential benefits?
